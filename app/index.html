Reading the key characters

The features used throughout this key, on a generic damselfly and dragonfly.

Eyes: touching along a seam or at a single point in most dragonflies; clearly separated (by more than one eye-width) in damselflies and in the clubtail family Gomphidae.
Pterostigma: the small coloured cell near the wingtip's leading edge. Its colour and length help separate similar species.
Cerci / appendages: the paired claspers at the tip of the male's abdomen. Their exact shape is often the only fully certain way to separate close relatives (e.g. hawkers, emeralds) — a hand lens or a sharp photo from behind helps.
Wing carriage at rest: folded along the abdomen (most damselflies), held half-open at about 45° (spreadwings, family Lestidae), or held flat out to the sides (all dragonflies).

A caution on colour

Colour patterns are useful but not absolute. They vary noticeably within a species and often change substantially with age. Males typically change colour more than females, and freshly emerged males often still show female-like colouring. Females occasionally show male-like colouring, and both sexes can turn up in unusual variants. Temperature affects colour too — a normally blue individual can look grey-brown in cool weather — and colours fade quickly after death, so a dried specimen looks quite different from a living one. Where a species pair is genuinely difficult, this key says so rather than pretending otherwise.
